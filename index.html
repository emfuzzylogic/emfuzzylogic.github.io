A Narrative Journey

Translating Stories: From a Chinese DVD Store to Global AI

By Emily Fan

The first time I heard about Netflix, I was seven years old in the only DVD store in my small North Chinese hometown. After our usual 1.5-hour bus ride across the city—the only way to access international films in 2005—the store owner told us about something that sounded impossible: "There's a company in America where people just mail a list of movies they want, and DVDs show up at their door. It's called Netflix."

On that long bus ride home, I tried to imagine how this worked. In my world, getting a movie required planning, patience, and an entire Saturday of travel. But Netflix had eliminated all that friction. That seed—the idea of using technology to bring stories directly to people who need them—would reshape how I thought about everything.

When Data Met Creativity

When we finally got internet three years later, I dove into an ocean of stories from everywhere—Russian films, French movies, Japanese animations, Korean dramas. Each spoke a language deeper than words. Great storytelling transcends borders, connecting human experiences across every cultural boundary.

Then came 2013, my final year of high school. I stumbled across House of Cards and couldn't figure out why a teenager from a small Chinese city who knew nothing about American politics was completely hooked. It bothered me for weeks—how did they know?

The answer changed everything: Netflix had used data to understand human preferences at scale, figured out how to use mathematics to help stories find their perfect audience. My love for patterns and my appetite for storytelling weren't separate paths—they were meant to work together.

Building Both Sides

In my province, 385,000 students took the college entrance exam that year, competing for limited spots at top universities. Ranking in the top 5% earned a place in a premier mathematics and statistics program—exactly what the plan required.

Except no professor had an answer when asked: "How could these statistical models work in filmmaking?"

The system didn't allow combining these fields. Two months of secret research into American universities. Three months later, first flight ever, heading to a university in western America with strong programs in both fields to study film production and statistics.

That first year was complete immersion—navigating life in a second language, learning how American film sets operated. I volunteered for over 15 film and theater productions, learning every aspect of production from script development to post-production workflows.

Second year. A student film set—twenty people, a borrowed camera, and a director who'd just gotten the call: lead actor sick, entire shooting day about to be lost.

Everyone stood around the equipment, waiting. The problem was obvious to anyone tracking the schedule, but restructuring on the fly required understanding both the creative vision—which scenes could be reordered without breaking the emotional arc—and the operational reality—which cast and locations were actually available.

"I have a solution," stepping forward with a revised schedule that redistributed scenes across the supporting cast. Three hours later, shooting resumed with a better structure than the original plan.

The best solutions exist where creative vision meets operational reality—a principle that would shape every project after.

The Language Bridge

In 2017, one of the leading AI research labs in Beijing—a pioneer in neural machine translation before the LLM era—was hiring for their language computing team. The position required PhD-level expertise: advanced statistical modeling, experimental design, rigorous evaluation methodologies.

As an undergraduate, the credential gap was real. But there was also a gap in their team: strong computer science backgrounds, but limited understanding of how translation actually serves human communication across cultures.

The application highlighted what the coursework had built—solid statistical foundations, hands-on experience designing and running experiments, rigorous evaluation methods from research projects. But it also explained something their existing team might be missing: years spent analyzing how different languages carry emotional weight differently, systematic observation of what gets lost when stories cross cultural boundaries, understanding of how translation products need to serve actual human needs, not just optimize accuracy metrics.

The lab hired someone who could bridge both worlds: the mathematical rigor to build reliable models and the cultural sensitivity to understand what those models should actually preserve.

For months, the work focused on training neural translation systems alongside researchers who'd been in this field for years. The core challenge: how do you teach a machine to understand context, tone, cultural references when there are no large language models to rely on? The team improved translation accuracy by 12% and reduced processing time by 11%—meaningful gains in 2017. But the deeper insight: technical metrics only matter if they serve the human experience you're trying to preserve. The best translation captures not just meaning, but feeling.

The Entertainment Industry Reality Check

After years of building technical skills—using machine learning to predict supply chain issues for thousands of retailers in Indonesia, developing AI products that served millions of users, working with companies across Korea, China, and the United States—a choice emerged.

The path in pure tech was clear: good opportunities, interesting problems, straightforward career progression. But the original goal hadn't changed. The intersection of technology and storytelling was where the work needed to happen.

So in 2021, when an opportunity came at an entertainment conglomerate's headquarters, the decision was made. Finally, a chance to apply everything learned directly to the entertainment industry—working between product, engineering, and data analytics teams, building predictive models for audience engagement, developing frameworks for content performance analysis.

For the first time, statistical modeling was being applied directly to storytelling at scale. This was it.

But in meeting after meeting, the same pattern emerged. Data would be collected, analyzed, presented—then set aside when decisions were made. The insights existed, but the culture wasn't ready to actually use them.

Within a year, it became clear: this wasn't the right fit. The company wasn't failing because they lacked resources. They were struggling because they couldn't evolve how decisions were made.

That experience crystallized something important: having the technology isn't enough. Having the data isn't enough. You need a culture that actually believes in using insights to make better creative decisions. You need people who understand that data doesn't replace creativity—it amplifies it.

The Pattern

Over nine years, a pattern emerged: every major project required bridging two worlds. Building AI products for American tech giants while consulting for companies across Korea, Indonesia, China. Creating tools for English-speaking markets while understanding how they'd need to adapt for Mandarin, Korean, Bahasa Indonesia.

The work deliberately built expertise across both American tech ecosystems and Asian markets. Fortune 500 companies taught how to operate in fast-paced Silicon Valley product cultures. Simultaneously, working with companies across different Asian markets revealed how products succeed or fail across radically different cultural contexts.

Deep involvement in both technology and creativity remained constant—continuing to work with the top independent film festival in the world's programming and film distribution since 2019, staying connected to how content is actually made, marketed, and distributed. Because you can't build tools for creators if you don't understand their workflows.

Products shipped that served millions of users across different markets—from AI chatbots to marketing platforms to predictive models for thousands of businesses. Building things that work at scale, across cultures, requires understanding both the technology and the human needs it serves.

The real value isn't just having worked in these markets—it's understanding the nuances that determine success. Korean audiences engage differently with content than Indonesian audiences. Advertising strategies that work in Shanghai fail in Jakarta. Measurement frameworks that make sense for US brands confuse Chinese advertisers. Language translation is table stakes; cultural translation is the competitive advantage.

Here's what nine years of building AI products across different markets revealed: personalization algorithms work beautifully within a single cultural context. But the moment you cross borders, the same recommendation engine that feels magical in one market feels tone-deaf in another.

The future of global streaming and advertising isn't just about better algorithms—it's about algorithms that understand cultural context, measurement frameworks that account for different consumption patterns, creative tools that empower local teams without requiring them to become data scientists.

This is particularly critical as streaming platforms invest billions in APAC markets while building advertising businesses from the ground up. The technical infrastructure might be the same globally, but the product, measurement, and creative strategies must be adapted for each market.

What Comes Next

The last decade has been preparation for when global entertainment platforms need people who can bridge technology, creativity, and cross-cultural understanding at scale.

The challenge: building systems that understand cultural context, creating measurement frameworks that account for different consumption patterns across markets, developing tools that empower local creative teams without requiring them to become data scientists.

The capabilities exist: technical depth to ship AI products that serve millions, creative sensitivity to understand what makes stories resonate, cross-cultural fluency to navigate APAC markets, product discipline to move from 0-to-1, and business acumen to connect technical capabilities to revenue and growth.

Fluency in both English and Mandarin Chinese matters, but more importantly, fluency in translating between American product thinking and Asian market realities—explaining complex technical systems to creative executives and explaining cultural preferences to data scientists.

The girl who first heard about Netflix in that cramped DVD store couldn't have imagined this moment. But every choice made was building toward something bigger: understanding how technology can help stories find the people who need them most, regardless of where those people live or what language they speak.

Like Frodo setting out from the Shire thinking he was just delivering a ring, the journey started with a simple goal and went further than imagined—from a small Chinese city to shipping AI products used by millions across multiple continents, from whispering on film sets to leading cross-functional teams, from undergraduate researcher to building the exact combination of skills that global entertainment platforms need right now.

The adventure continues. The infrastructure that brings stories to billions is waiting to be built—not just by streaming content, but by ensuring those stories resonate across every cultural boundary, just like those international films did for a little girl on a bus in North China twenty years ago.
